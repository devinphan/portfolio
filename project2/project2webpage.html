📊 Project 2: Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

👩‍💻 Taylor Chen — t2chen@ucsd.edu
📌 PROPOSITION
Visualizations emphasizing local, short-term climate impacts are more persuasive than global long-term trends, but risk becoming deceptive without proper context.

✅ FOR the Proposition

persuasive design · FOR Local & short-term framing increases urgency
  • Rationale 1: Geographic anchoring (local maps + city names) triggers psychological proximity — viewers see “their own backyard” at risk.
  • Rationale 2: Short 15-year baseline emphasizes recent acceleration, making abstract warming feel tangible and urgent.
  • Rationale 3: Concrete annotations (e.g., “40% increase in local wildfires”) create narrative drive, boosting emotional engagement.

❌ AGAINST the Proposition

counter argument · AGAINST Deceptive risks: omission, cherry-picking & misleading scales
  • Critique 1: Short timeframes hide natural climate variability, potentially misrepresenting signal magnitude.
  • Critique 2: Ignoring global baseline (e.g., satellite temperature record) makes local charts deceptive — viewers miss the full picture.
  • Critique 3: Axis manipulation (non-zero start, log scales without notice) or spatial cherry-picking creates persuasive-but-false narratives.

📝 Final Reflection

Where persuasion ends and deception begins: This project revealed a crucial tension: effective data communication often relies on emphasis, framing, and simplification — the very tools that can also mislead. My “FOR” visualization (Figure 1) successfully made climate change feel urgent by zooming into local data over a 15-year window. But the “AGAINST” critique (Figure 2) demonstrates that without a multi-decadal baseline, clear axis labels, and contextual notes, the exact same visual strategy becomes deceptive. A viewer might believe the trend is unprecedented when longer records show similar past fluctuations.

Lessons on ethical design: I learned that transparency is the difference between persuasion and deception. An ethical persuasive visualization should include: (1) a clearly stated baseline period, (2) global or long-term context as a small multiple or annotation, (3) zero-origin axes for bar charts, and (4) uncertainty indicators where appropriate. In my revised design, I added a small sparkline of global temperature trends alongside the local chart — preserving persuasive impact while protecting against omission-based deception.

Takeaways for future work: The most important question designers must ask is: “What am I leaving out?”. If the answer includes critical context that would change audience interpretation, the visualization crosses into deceptive territory. For DSC 106 and beyond, I will always pair local, emotive framing with transparent annotations about data sources, baseline choices, and uncertainty. Persuasion and integrity are not mutually exclusive — honest captions and linked raw data build trust while still telling a compelling story.
